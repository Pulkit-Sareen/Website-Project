What are Carbon Emissions?

Carbon emissions refer to the release of carbon dioxide (CO₂) and other carbon compounds into the atmosphere, primarily through human activities. These emissions are a major contributor to climate change and global warming. The term "carbon emissions" is often used interchangeably with "greenhouse gas emissions," although it specifically focuses on carbon-based gases.

Sources of Carbon Emissions

Carbon emissions originate from various sources, both natural and anthropogenic (human-caused):

Fossil Fuel Combustion: Burning coal, oil, and natural gas for electricity, heat, and transportation is the largest source of carbon emissions. This includes emissions from power plants, vehicles, and industrial processes.
Deforestation:Cutting down trees reduces the planet's capacity to absorb CO₂, leading to higher levels of carbon in the atmosphere.
Agriculture: Agricultural practices, including livestock farming and rice cultivation, produce significant amounts of methane (CH₄), a potent greenhouse gas.
Industrial Processes: Manufacturing cement, steel, and chemicals releases substantial amounts of CO₂ and other greenhouse gases.
Natural Sources: While natural processes like respiration, volcanic eruptions, and wildfires also emit CO₂, these emissions are typically balanced by natural carbon sinks, such as forests and oceans.

Low Emission Levels

Definition: Low carbon emission levels per person are typically considered to be below 2 metric tons of CO₂ per year.

Medium Emission Levels

Definition: Medium carbon emission levels per person range from 2 to 8 metric tons of CO₂ per year.

High Emission Levels

Definition:High carbon emission levels per person are considered to be above 8 metric tons of CO₂ per year.
